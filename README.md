# Case report: sleep/wake pattern between timezone analysis

Jet lag is a recognised sleep disorder that results from traversing time zones at a rate exceeding the circadian clock's ability to adapt. Current literature indicates that recovery from jet lag is contingent upon the number of time zones crossed, as the circadian clock typically adjusts by approximately one hour per day, although advancing more than a few time zones remains more challenging. The severity of jet lag symptoms also varies based on travel direction, with eastward travel generally inducing more pronounced desynchronisation. Eastward flights generally induce more severe jet lag, as the human body experiences difficulty in advancing its circadian rhythm. Furthermore, frequent flights have been associated with an increased risk of jet lag, and older individuals tend to exhibit greater susceptibility to circadian misalignment.
This longitudinal study offers real-world insights into the sleep-wake patterns of a 48-year-old healthy woman who, for professional reasons, travelled across a single time zone (Italy to the United Kingdom and vice versa) every two weeks for over two years (2022–2024). She continuously wore an actigraph to monitor her sleep patterns, reporting only early evening somnolence while in the United Kingdom. Given the limited research on habitual travellers crossing a single time zone, these findings contribute valuable empirical data, improving our understanding of the nuanced effects of regular travel on sleep-wake regulation and circadian stability.
